ANTI-MORTGAGE FRAUD AND PROPERTY FRAUD POLICY

 

1.     Role of the Money Laundering Reporting Office (“MLRO”)

The firm’s precautions to avoid involvement in mortgage frauds and other property frauds are supervised by the firm’s MLRO.  Questions and concerns about possible frauds should be referred to her.

 

2.     Frauds

Fee earners handling property transactions must stay alert to the risk of fraud.  We may be liable to clients and may incur criminal or professional liability if we fail to spot or report frauds.  Possible frauds include but are not limited to the following.

2.1.  Large scale and organised mortgage frauds, in which criminals obtain large loans which are not repaid.  Such frauds may involve the use of false names, nominees, shell companies corrupt professionals who provide inflated valuations, and/or rapid re-sales at increased prices (known as “flipping”).  The buy-to-let market is particularly vulnerable to organised mortgage fraud.

In one variant fraudsters first purchase a property (often using criminal funds to buy a cheap repossessed property) then obtain a mortgage advance and disappear.

2.2.  Opportunistic mortgage frauds, in which otherwise honest people deceive the lender in some way.  For example they may not reveal that they are taking out a second loan, are intending to let out the property, or are taking a cashback from the seller.

2.3.  Application hijacking. Criminals intervene before completion of a mortgage, falsely claiming to be new solicitors acting for the purchaser.  The criminals generally take the details of someone who is on the professional register.

2.4.  Equity release frauds, typically a sale at an undervalue purporting to be an equity release scheme. The seller may be told that they will be able to stay in the property for a low or no rent and/or that they will receive further payments of equity in the future and/or be able to buy the property back/pay off the money advanced in the future. In reality the seller simply becomes a tenant. Those advising on or selling equity release products must be FCA regulated.

2.5.  Sale and rent back agreements, in which owners are induced to sell their homes and rent them back, usually to resolve a mortgage arrears problem. Such deals are not necessarily objectionable, but some owners are tricked into terrible deals.

Anyone offering a sale and rent back scheme must be FCA regulated. Acting in sale and rent back cases is high risk and you need to consider the extra work that will be involved in complying with your regulatory obligations.

Particularly guard against schemes such as Exchange and Delayed Completion “EDC” where the promoter of the scheme asserts they do not need to be regulated because the sale has not completed.

2.6.  Fake law firms.  Various frauds depend on criminals setting up fake law firms, or copying the details of genuine firms.

2.7.  Vacant property frauds.  Criminals may obtain a default judgement for a non-existent debt against the registered owner of vacant property.  Thereafter they may obtain a charging order and sell the property.

2.8.  Deceased estate frauds. Criminals may target properties following the death of the owner e.g. when there are no known heirs or probate has been delayed. They may claim to be an heir, possibly forging a will, or pose as the deceased person. They may then sell the property or seek a mortgage and then disappear.

2.9.  Impersonation frauds.  People impersonate the owner(s) of a property, and so succeed in selling it or raising a mortgage loan. For example:

·       following a divorce one spouse may sell the former matrimonial home without informing their co-owner, often using their new partner to impersonate their co-owner;

·       a crook may impersonate the owner of vacant property;

·       a tenant may impersonate their landlord and sell property they have rented.

 

3.     Precautions

When handling property transactions take the following precautions.

3.1.  Check other firms

If they are not already known to you, check that the lawyers acting for the other party are who they claim.  This may involve the following steps.

3.1.1.     Click on the SRA digital badge which should be on the website of an SRA regulated firm (from mid-2019).

3.1.2.     Check the Law Society “Find a Solicitor” service or the Directory of Licensed Conveyancers as appropriate.

3.1.3.     In cases of doubt, also check the details with the SRA (whose contact number is 0370 6062555) or the Council of Licensed Conveyancers.

3.1.4.     Check that the details and the final destination of documents match the details held by the regulator.

3.1.5.     Contact the firm directly using the contact details proved by the professional body, since some crooks assume the identity of firms that actually exist.

3.2.  Assess risk and respond accordingly

The firm’s file opening process requires you to assess the risk of fraud, as well as the risk of money laundering.

 

Ask questions if you receive unusual instructions or there are inconsistencies or other warning signs, such as an anomaly in the valuation of the property.

3.3.  Check clients’ identity scrupulously

Standard anti-money laundering checks may detect fraudsters who are impersonating property owners, or who are using nominees or shell companies to buy and sell property.  Take particular care if we do not meet the client. Look for anomalies in any documents you see (for example if someone’s date of birth indicates he is too young to be the person he claims to be).

3.4.  Check the seller in particular

To address the risk that the seller is an impostor, a conveyancer acting for a seller may seek additional evidence to confirm their identity. For example, ask them to produce documents which only the true owner would have, such as conveyancing papers from the purchase, or receipts for renovation work. It may also be appropriate to insist on meeting the client and keeping a photo of their appearance.

The following factors indicate a higher risk that the seller may be an impostor.

·       The property is high value.

·       The property is unmortgaged.

·       The property has been vacant or let.

·       The sale is a probate sale by someone who is not clearly a member of the deceased proprietor’s family.

·       The seller seeks a rapid completion for no good reason.

·       The seller is reluctant to attend for identification in person.

              If you act for a seller and two or more of those factors are present seek additional evidence to verify their identity.

              If you act for a buyer and risk factors are present, you should ensure that

·       we are provided with evidence to confirm the seller’s identity, and/or

·       the conveyancers acting for the seller are regulated and accept without qualification the obligations that apply under the Law Society’s Code for Completion by Post.

3.5.  Check signatures

3.5.1.     Only witness signatures if you have actually seen the person signing the document.

3.5.2.     If any document has been pre-signed, either verify it was pre-signed in the presence of a witness or have it re-signed in your presence.

3.5.3.     Compare signatures on transaction documents with other available documentation if you notice a discrepancy or you have concerns about the identity of any of the parties.

3.6.  Be alert to risk factors

This note has explained various risk factors. In addition, bear in mind the following risk factors.

· The client and/or property are a long distance from the firm.

· The client seems uninterested in the purchase.

· The seller is a private company, or a recent seller was a private company.

· The client does not usually engage in property investment of this scale.

· The owner has owned the property for less than six months.

· The client's credit history is short.

· Sub-sale or back-to-back transactions.

· A last-minute change of representative on the other side.

· The property has a history of being re-sold or re-mortgaged quickly.

· A transfer of title to land is requested with respect to only some of the seller's holdings and the properties are not grouped together.

· Finance is sought after the property has been registered in the buyer's name.

· The owner appears to be too young given their supposed length of ownership.

· The other conveyancer has an e-mail address from a web-based provider.

· A County Court judgment against the property.

· The land is transferred following a court order, but the mortgage is sought some time later.

· The property value has increased out of line with the market.

· The seller or developer has provided incentives, allowances or discounts.

· The deposit is being paid by someone other than the purchaser.

· The purchaser has paid the deposit directly to the seller.

· You are asked to pay surplus funds to someone you do not know, or to the introducer.

· You are asked to record an inflated price.

· There has been a recent transfer at less than full market value.

· You are encouraged to act for both buyer and seller.

3.7.  Report

If in doubt check the position, normally by speaking with the firm’s MLRO.  Thereafter decisions about what we do should be made by the MLRO, not by individual fee earners.  However the principles are these.

3.7.1.     We must keep our lender client informed of relevant facts, so far as our duty of confidentiality to the borrower client allows.

3.7.2.     We may be released from our duty of confidentiality if we are satisfied that the client is using the firm to further a fraud (e.g. because a client has made deliberate misrepresentations on their mortgage application).

3.7.3.     If a conflict arises between lender and borrower clients we may need to cease to act.

3.7.4.     The firm may need to make a money laundering report to the National Crime Agency.

3.7.5.     We must avoid tipping off a potential suspect that a report has been made.
