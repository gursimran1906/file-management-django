ANTI-TAX EVASION POLICY

 

This document has been approved by the senior management of the firm. It is important that you read and apply it.

The persons responsible for this policy is the firm’s Compliance Officer for Legal Practice and Money Laundering Reporting Officer. In general that is to whom you should report any concerns you have about possible tax evasion, or to whom you should turn with questions. Junior members of staff may alternatively raise their concerns with their supervisor.

WARNING

Failure to comply with this policy may be treated as gross misconduct and result in disciplinary action, in addition to any other professional or criminal sanctions which may apply.

 

1.    OUR COMMITMENT

1.1. As a firm we are committed to doing business ethically and in accordance with our professional and legal obligations. Our commitment to avoiding facilitating tax evasion is one aspect of that.

1.2. Moreover under Part 3 of the Criminal Finances Act 2017 the firm is expected to have reasonable procedures to prevent the criminal facilitation of tax evasion by staff or other associated persons. Without such procedures the firm may be exposed to criminal sanctions. This document explains those procedures.

2.    WHAT WE EXPECT OF YOU, AND WHY

2.1. Your work for the firm may present you with opportunities to facilitate tax evasion by clients or others. We expect you to resist any temptation to engage in such behaviour, and to report any improper behaviour by others.

2.2. If you were to facilitate tax evasion you would commit a serious criminal offence. You would also leave yourself open to professional sanctions and internal disciplinary action. Moreover you could expose the firm to liability.

3.    WHAT IS “FACILITATING TAX EVASION”?

3.1. Tax evasion is fraudulent activity. It is quite different from tax avoidance (the lawful minimisation of tax liability). It is also different from honest errors in tax affairs, even when such errors amount to an offence.

3.2. To be more specific, fraudulent activity intended to divert funds from the public revenue constitutes the common law offence of cheating the public revenue. There are also statutory offences of “fraudulently evading” various taxes. It is not necessary that any tax actually be successfully evaded.

3.3. Facilitating tax evasion is a crime, if done deliberately and dishonestly. That can apply even if you are outside the UK at the time. Facilitating the evasion of foreign tax may likewise be a crime. It is not necessary for your client to be convicted for you to face conviction.

4.    YOUR DUTY TO REPORT

4.1. It is not enough merely to avoid personally facilitating tax evasion. As with all aspects of your professional work, you should be open, report any concerns about questionable behaviour, past or present, and if in doubt, seek guidance.

4.2. If someone within or outside the firm suggests anything which might be regarded as tax evasion, report it.

5.    WHISTLE-BLOWER PROTECTION: OUR PROMISE TO YOU

5.1. You will not be subject to any retaliation or retribution for reporting in the public interest your concerns about a suspected breach of this policy.

5.2. Anyone who retaliates against someone who has made such a report will be subject to disciplinary action.

5.3. While we would prefer you to report any concerns openly, you may do so anonymously.

5.4. Whistle-blower protection applies even if your concerns turn out to be unjustified, so long as you made the report reasonably believing it to be in the public interest. It may not apply in other circumstances, for example if someone maliciously makes allegations which they know to be false.

6.    EXAMPLES

The following are non-exhaustive examples where opportunities to facilitate tax evasion might arise within our practice areas, and to which you should be alert.

6.1. In any practice area:

a.        Delivering a misleading or inaccurate bill which enables a client to evade tax. For example billing a company for work actually done for its directors or shareholders, to assist clients to claim a tax deduction to which they are not entitled.

6.2. Property:

a.        Preparing documents which misstate the price of property to enable a client to eva1de stamp duty land tax or capital gains tax.

b.        Assisting in property being bought using nominees or other structures so that a client may illegally avoid tax.

6.3. Corporate:

a.        Assisting in creating corporate or trust structures designed to conceal a client’s taxable income or assets.

b.        Referring clients to third parties in tax havens to set up accounts or structures which you know are to facilitate tax evasion, rather than tax avoidance.

c.         Using side letters so that aspects of a transaction with taxable effects are not apparent from reading the main agreement.

6.4. Wills and Probate:

a.        Assisting in creating trust or other structures designed to conceal a client’s taxable income or assets.

b.        Giving clients advice on how to make lifetime transfers in ways which HMRC will find difficult to detect.

c.        Preparing documents which misstate the value of an estate in order to avoid inheritance tax.

6.5. Family Law:

a.        Helping a client to put forward figures which you know to be false will involve a number of offences, possibly including conspiracy to pervert the course of justice and tax evasion offences.

b.        Clients who have been ordered to disclose their earnings in connection with family proceedings may tell you that they have committed tax evasion in the past. That information will normally be privileged, and is not normally reportable under money laundering law. However in such cases discuss the matter with the MLRO or COLP to ensure that your involvement in the case will not create any liabilities or concerns.
