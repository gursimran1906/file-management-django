CONFLICTS POLICY

Background

The general rule is that we must not act if

·       there is a conflict of interests, or a significant risk of a conflict, or

·       we hold material confidential information for a past or present client, and that confidentiality would be put at risk by us acting.

In this note the word “conflicts” refers not only to conflicts of interest in the strict sense, but also to situations that put confidentiality at risk.

We must identify conflicts swiftly and make appropriate decisions about them. The SRA requires us to have effective systems and controls in place to enable us to identify and deal with conflicts of interests. Further, unidentified or mismanaged conflicts can do serious damage to client relationships and to our firm's reputation.  Compliance with our conflicts policy is important, and compulsory for everyone.

If you are in any doubt about your obligations consult the Compliance Officer for Legal Practice.

Types of Conflict

In deciding whether we will act, or continue to act, the following types of conflict and related risk must be considered.

1.         Client Conflicts

            This is any situation where you owe separate duties to act in the best interests of two or more clients in relation to the same or related matters, and those duties conflict, or there is a significant risk that those duties may conflict.

            We may sometimes be able to act despite a client conflict if the clients have a substantially common interest, or if the conflict arises only because they are competing for the same asset.  However various conditions must be satisfied.  See the note below on “substantially common interest”, which is the most common exception in practice.

2.         Confidential Information

            You must not act for A in a matter where A has an interest adverse to B, and B is a client for whom you hold confidential information which is material to A in that matter, unless the confidential information can be protected by the use of safeguards and various conditions are satisfied. Information remains confidential even if we are not currently acting for B.

3.         Conflicts Arising in Existing Matters

            If a conflict arises during a matter, please notify a director of this firm without delay.  We will generally need to cease acting.  We will normally only be able to continue to act for one client if we do not hold material confidential information for the other.

4.         Own Interest Conflicts

            This is defined as any situation where your duty to act in the best interests of any client in relation to a matter conflicts, or there is a significant risk that it may conflict, with your own interests in relation to that or a related matter. 

5.         Potential Conflicts

            As noted above, we cannot generally act if there is a significant risk of a conflict.

6.         Commercial Conflicts

            Although it is not professionally improper for us to act in litigation against a current or past client, where we hold no material confidential information for that client, we will normally decline to do so.  That is mainly in the interests of good client relations, but it also avoids any argument that we have confidential information about how the client approaches his or her legal affairs, as a result of our previous work.  Likewise a business client may be unhappy if we were also to advise a competitor even though we are not acting for them on a related matter.

Substantially Common Interest

We may act for two or more clients although there is a client conflict because they have a substantially common interest.  For example two individuals buying an asset together may ask us to draft an agreement setting out their mutual rights.  It may in such situations be disproportionate, for example in terms of cost and speed of response, for them to engage separate solicitors.

Substantially common interest applies where there is a clear common purpose in relation to any matter or a particular aspect of it between the clients and a strong consensus on how it is to be achieved and the client conflict is peripheral to this common purpose.

However this exception is dependent upon:

·       us having explained the relevant issues and risks to the clients, and having a reasonable belief that they understand

·       all the clients giving informed consent in writing

·       it being reasonable for us to act for all the clients

·       it being in their best interests, and

·       the benefits to the clients of us acting outweighing the risks.

Please note that it may be unreasonable to act because the clients have unequal bargaining power.

If we are acting for two or more clients we must always keep the position under review.  For example, if one of the conditions set out above is no longer fulfilled then we must cease acting for one or both clients.

 

Conflict Checks 

When opening a file, please check the names against our database to establish if there appears to be a conflict.  Please complete the form carefully, because our checks depend upon it.

Equally importantly, if during the matter additional parties become involved, or the substance of the matter changes, you must notify a director of this firm.  They will then carry out fresh checks.

General Advice Files

General advice files can lead to conflicts going undetected. They may only be used where we give ongoing legal advice to a client in circumstances where there are no adverse parties. General advice files must never be used for contentious or transactional work.

New Members of Staff

When lawyers join us from other law firms we take reasonable care to identify matters upon which they have worked in the past which may create a conflicts problem (or more accurately, a confidential information problem).  However such checks may not detect all possible conflicts.  New members of staff, and those working with them, should take particular care to identify such problems at an early stage.

Own Interest Conflicts

You must consider whether your ability as an individual, or that of anyone within the firm, to act in the best interests of the client is impaired by any financial interest, a personal relationship, the appointment of you, or a member of the firm or your family, to public office, commercial relationships or your employment.  If you are aware of any such issue, either decline to act, or discuss the matter with a director of this firm.

You are expected to be alert to any situation which may put you or the firm in a difficult position.  Here are just a few examples.

·       If a relative or another employee or partner of the firm has an interest in the matter that may create an “own interest” conflict.

·       In a personal capacity, selling to or buying from, lending to or borrowing from a client, unless the client has obtained “independent legal advice” is contrary to the SRA Standards and Regulations.

·       We must properly account to clients for any financial benefit we receive as a result of our instructions.

·       You should avoid acting in litigation if it is clear that you, or anyone within our firm, will be called as a witness in the matter, unless you are satisfied that this will not prejudice your independence as an advocate, or litigator, or the interests of your clients or the interests of justice.

·       Taking unfair advantage of a public office held by you, or a member of your family, or a member of your firm or their family is contrary to the SRA Standards and Regulations.

·       Gifts can create problems.  Avoid any situation in which anyone could suppose that there was a risk of someone’s behaviour being influenced improperly by a gift being given or received.  See the firm’s Gifts Policy, which includes further guidance on this subject.
